How much can you ask for?

You must strike a balance, requiring a security deposit that sufficiently insures against potential damage but that does not overburden your new tenants. Although some states do not impose limits on deposits, several states maintain strict requirements to ensure equal opportunities for housing. In states with a security deposit limit, the most common maximum amount is a sum equal to two months’ rent. Other states limit deposits to one and one-half months’ rent or to a “reasonable amount” given the property profile, pet policy and other relevant factors.

How should funds be held?

Security deposits remain the legal property of the tenant until a triggering event, such as damage to the unit, occurs. In other words, you are simply a caretaker of the money — not an owner — and must take proper precautions to ensure security funds are not allotted to other operational costs or unlawfully spent. Although not every state requires a separate bank account, many states require that landlords keep tenant funds in an escrow-type trust protected from creditors and clearly earmarked as tenant security deposit funds. Some states, like Georgia for example, require landlords to either keep security deposits in a separate account or obtain a surety bond.

Another issue to consider is what to do with any interest that accrues on tenant funds held in a checking or savings account. While many state statutes do not directly address this issue, the following states and jurisdictions require landlords to return interest to the tenant: Connecticut, District of Columbia, Florida, Illinois, Iowa, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New Hampshire, New Mexico, New Jersey, New York, North Dakota, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and Virginia. In these places, rules vary in terms of the length of time the tenant must reside on the property to earn interest and in terms of the amount of the underlying deposit.

When can you keep the security deposit?

Your lease agreement should clearly spell out which situations would warrant retaining the tenant’s security deposit; otherwise, the tenant may have a strong case against you for wrongful withholding of tenant funds. Nonetheless, many state statutes outline reasons why the landlord may withhold funds, even if those reasons are not explicitly cited in the lease and even if the tenant never signed a lease to begin with. If tenants ask you, “what is a security deposit” and what it covers, let them know that legitimate reasons for withholding may include the following:

  1. Failure to pay rent
  2. Damage to the property
  3. Damages exceeding normal wear and tear
  4. Unpaid utilities
  5. Increased real estate taxes (if you’ve included a tax-escalation clause in your lease)
  6. Removal of abandoned property
  7. Cleaning costs

In nearly all states, you must provide an itemized list in writing detailing the costs of the expenses. If the tenant disputes the list, he or she may initiate a lawsuit in the jurisdiction’s landlord-tenant court, which would require you to prove that damage occurred and that repair costs were congruent with the amount withheld from the security deposit.

How soon should you return the deposit?

You must return unused security deposit funds in a timely manner, keeping in mind that these funds legally belong to the tenant unless a triggering event has occurred. Each state maintains its own deadline for the return of deposit monies, which generally ranges from 14 to 45 days. Usually, the clock starts ticking after the tenant has moved out and the landlord has had an opportunity to inspect the property.
